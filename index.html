Learn the gas laws

Six simple rules sit under everything in this tool. Tap one to open its toy: drag the sliders and watch a box of gas particles obey the rule.

What a gas really is

The one picture that unlocks every rule below.

A gas is a huge crowd of tiny balls, far too small to see, flying around and bouncing off the walls. That one picture unlocks everything below. Three words to learn:

Pressure is the drumming of the balls on the walls. More hits, or harder hits, means more push.

Temperature is how fast the balls fly. Hot means fast, cold means slow.

Volume is the size of the room they bounce in.

How hot (ball speed)
Size of the room
Push on the walls: 40 (a score, not a unit)
Try this: speed the balls up, or squeeze the room smaller. Either way they hammer the walls more, and the push score climbs. Every law below is just this toy with one thing pinned still.

Boyle's law

Squeeze it, the push rises.

Keep the balls at the same speed but shrink their room. They cross it quicker, so they hit the walls more often. Half the room, double the push. A bike pump with your thumb over the end is exactly this. The rule:

P1 × V1 = P2 × V2push before × room before = push after × room after. The answer never changes.
Room for the gas
Started: 100 kPa of push in 10 litres of room. Now: 7.0 litres, 143 kPa
100 × 10 = 143 × 7.0 = 1,000 always
Try this: drag the room down to 5.0 litres, exactly half of where it started. Watch the piston wall glow warmer as the room shrinks, and the push lands on exactly 200 kPa, exactly double. That is the law working.

At work: a vacuum pump does the opposite, it gives the gas a bigger room so the push falls. The vacuum, compressor and pump-down tabs all lean on this.

Charles's law

Heat it, it grows.

Speed the balls up but let the room stretch, like a balloon. Faster balls need more space to keep the same push, so the gas grows. A balloon left in a hot car puffs up. The rule:

V1 / T1 = V2 / T2room divided by temperature stays the same. Temperature must be in Kelvin.
Why Kelvin? Zero degrees Celsius is not zero heat, the balls are still flying. Kelvin counts from truly stone cold, minus 273 degrees Celsius, where the balls stop. Just add 273 to Celsius. Only in Kelvin does doubling the number mean truly doubling the heat, which is why the formula insists on it.
Temperature
Started: 5.0 litres at 0 °C (273 K). Now: 100 °C (373 K), room 6.8 litres
5.0 / 273 = 6.8 / 373 = 0.018 always
Try this: drag all the way to 273 °C. That is 546 K, double the Kelvin it started at, and the room lands on 10.0 litres, exactly double, while the balls visibly speed up too. Notice it does not double at 273 °C on the Celsius side alone, only the Kelvin doubling counts.

At work: warm gas at a pump inlet takes up more room than the cool reference on a datasheet. The flow converter corrects for exactly this.

Gay-Lussac's law

Heat a sealed gas, the push rises.

Now weld the room shut so it cannot grow, and speed the balls up. They hit the walls faster and harder, so the push climbs instead. This is why an aerosol can warns you not to heat it. The rule:

P1 / T1 = P2 / T2push divided by temperature stays the same. Temperature in Kelvin again.
Temperature
Started: 100 kPa at 0 °C (273 K). Now: 100 °C (373 K), push 137 kPa
100 / 273 = 137 / 373 = 0.37 always
Try this: drag to 273 °C, double the Kelvin. The sealed canister cannot grow, so watch the gauge needle swing round and the walls glow warmer instead, and the push lands on exactly 200 kPa, double. Same shape of rule as Charles, just with push growing instead of room.

At work: it is why discharge gas comes out warmer than it went in, and why sealed-up gas and heat together are a safety matter.

The combined gas law

All three at once.

Real jobs change room and temperature at the same time, so Boyle, Charles and Gay-Lussac get rolled into one rule. This is the exact rule the flow converter, vacuum and compressor tabs use to turn a datasheet's reference flow into the real flow at the inlet. The rule:

P1 V1 / T1 = P2 V2 / T2push × room, divided by temperature, stays the same. Kelvin as always.
Room for the gas
Temperature
Started: 100 kPa, 10 litres, 0 °C. Now: room 10.0 L, temperature 0 °C, push 100 kPa
100 × 10 / 273 = 100 × 10.0 / 273 = 3.66 always
Try this: shrink the room to 5.0 litres and heat to 100 °C at the same time. Squeezing doubles the push, heating adds more again, and it lands on about 273 kPa. One rule handled both changes at once.

The ideal gas law

The full picture.

The parent of everything above. It adds the one thing the others left out: how much gas is actually in the room, the size of the crowd. The rule:

P V = n R Tpush × room = amount of gas × a fixed number × temperature

P the push (pressure)

V the room (volume)

n how many balls are in the crowd (the amount of gas, counted in moles)

R a fixed number, 8.314, that just makes the units agree. Never changes.

T the temperature, in Kelvin

Size of the crowd (n)
Balls in the room: 16. Push on the walls: 100 (a score, not a unit)
Try this: drag the crowd up to 40 balls, room and speed both left alone. More balls means more hits on the walls every second, so the push score climbs on its own, no squeezing or heating needed. Pin the amount of gas still and this collapses into the combined law above, which is why you rarely calculate with it directly here.

Dalton's law

Shares of a damp gas.

Mix two gases in one room, say air balls and water balls, and each crowd does its own drumming on the walls as if the other were not there. The total push is simply the two pushes added up. And here is the useful bit: warmer water pushes harder, so hot damp gas carries a bigger share of water. The rule:

Ptotal = Pdry + Pwatereach gas pushes its own share, and the shares add up to the total
Temperature
At 40 °C: dry gas pushes 93.9 kPa (teal balls), water pushes 7.4 kPa (orange balls), together 101.3. Water's share: 7.3 percent
Try this: slide up to 60 °C and watch orange water balls join the crowd as their share climbs to nearly a fifth of the whole gas. That is why hot saturated gas needs a noticeably bigger pump than the dry figure suggests.

At work: this is exactly how the vapour and wet gas tab splits a saturated flow into dry gas and water.

Normal / Actual flow

Also called Standard to actual, or Nm3/h to m3/h. This is the calculator that turns the Normal cubes on a datasheet into real cubes.
What it isGas is squishy. The same amount of it takes up more or less room depending on how warm it is and how much pressure it is under.
Why it mattersA datasheet describes a gas flow at one agreed set of conditions, called Normal or Standard. A pump deals with the gas as it really is at the inlet, where it may be warmer or under vacuum and take up a different amount of room. A pump curve is drawn in the real amount of room, so you convert first or you size the wrong pump.
Where you would use itA customer asks for 1000 cubic metres an hour at the Normal reference, but the gas is at 40 degrees and under a little vacuum. Before you pick a pump you turn that into the real room it takes up, which might be 1300. You size on the 1300.
Uses: Combined gas law

Conveying sizing

Moving powder or granules down a pipe on a stream of air. Answers the three quoting questions in one go: how much air, how hard to pull or push, and which machine family.
What it isPneumatic conveying floats product along a pipe in fast-moving air, like leaves in a strong gust. You can suck it along with a vacuum pump, or blow it along with a blower. This tab covers dilute phase, the common style where the product flies in the air stream.
Why it mattersGet the air speed right and the product sails clean down the line. Too slow and it drops out and blocks the pipe. Ask for more pull or push than the machine family can give and it never makes the rate. Pipe size, air, pressure and machine all hang together, so they must be worked out together.
Where you would use itA customer tips bulk bags of powder into a hopper and wants it on top of a silo 30 metres of pipe away. You need a budget answer today: which pump, what pipe, roughly what motor.
Uses: Combined gas law + conveying rules of thumb

Fill these in

Rate = amount each day / hours available (cubic metres become tonnes using the loose weight).

Working length = level pipe + climb + 8 m per bend.

Air needed = rate / product-to-air ratio, then checked against the pipe: the pipe is rounded up to a standard size and the air topped up so the pickup speed is never below your minimum. The final ratio shown is worked back from the real air.

Pressure = pipe friction for air × (1 + 0.7 × ratio) for the product's share, + the weight of product in the riser, + getting it up to speed, + a filter allowance, all + 20% margin. Solved a few times over because pressure and air speed feed each other.

Which machine

Describe the job and this ranks the machine technologies that suit it, with reasons, plus a very rough motor size. Technology first, brand catalogues later.
What it isEvery pump and blower technology has a sweet spot: how deep or hard it can work, and what sort of gas it can stomach. Side channel and lobe blowers like shallow work and big flows. Claws love dusty conveying. Oiled vanes go deep on clean gas. Liquid rings shrug off wet, filthy and flammable gas. Dry screws go deepest of all, oil free, at a price.
Why it mattersMost bad selections come from technology mismatch, not sizing: an oiled vane pump drowning in vapour, a blower asked to pull deeper than it can, a dry pump eating corrosive gas. Pick the right family first and the sizing is the easy part.
Where you would use itA customer describes a job on the phone. Before opening any catalogue, punch in the pressure, the flow and what is in the gas, and you know which technologies to talk about and which to steer away from.
Uses: operating windows + gas compatibility + isothermal power

Fill these in

Vacuum means pulling gas out of something, working below atmosphere. Compression means pushing gas somewhere, working above it.
For a vacuum job: the absolute pressure the process must reach, where 1013 mbar is atmosphere and 0 is perfect vacuum, so smaller means deeper. For a compression job: how much above atmosphere it must push, so 500 mbar here means half a bar of boost. Same box, two meanings, the job type above decides which.
How much gas must be moved, measured at the working pressure. If you only know free air, the Flow tab converts it.
The single biggest decider. Wet means water vapour, steam or condensing liquids coming through with the gas. Solvents and flammables means anything that burns or explodes. Corrosive means acid gases and the like. If several apply, pick the nastiest one.
Oil lubricated machines breathe a fine oil mist out the exhaust. Fine for a workshop, not fine for food, pharma, clean rooms or anywhere the exhaust touches product. If in doubt, say it must be oil free.
Continuous means running for hours on end. Stop-start means short bursts with rests. Most technologies handle both, but it sharpens the reasoning.

Where you would use itA pump is short on vacuum. Clock the motor pulley and the pump pulley with a tape measure, read the motor's nameplate speed, and this tells you what speed the pump should be turning at. Measure the real speed with a tachometer and compare.

Reference & words

Plain words

Gauge pressure: measured against the air around you. Zero means the same as the air. In this app it is for readings above the air, typed as a plus.

Absolute pressure: measured from a full vacuum, from nothing at all. Always a plus. The math uses this inside.

Vacuum: below the surrounding air. Entered here as a minus Gauge number, for example -20.

Normal or Standard flow (Nm3/h): a fixed set of conditions used to describe an amount of gas so it can be compared, no matter the real conditions. Usually zero degrees and normal air pressure.

Actual flow (m3/h): the room the gas truly takes up at the inlet, warm or under vacuum.

Compression ratio: how many times tighter the gas is squeezed, outlet pressure divided by inlet pressure.

Inside a liquid ring pump

Watch the water ring do the pumping.

A bladed wheel spins inside a round casing, mounted off centre. Water gets slung out to the wall and forms a spinning ring, and because the wheel sits off centre, the pockets of gas trapped between the blades and the water grow on one side and shrink on the other. Growing pockets suck gas in. Shrinking pockets squeeze it out. The water is the piston, and it never wears out.

Speed
Green port: pockets growing, gas pulled in. Orange port: pockets pinched shut, gas pushed out, with the blade tips dipping back into the ring to seal each pocket.
Try this: slow the slider right down and follow one pocket of teal gas all the way round. It fills out on the green side, gets pinched smaller and smaller as it comes past the top, and empties out on the orange side. That single lap is the whole pump.

At work: the ring happily swallows the vapour, dirt and flammable gas that kills dry machines, which is why the Which machine tab reaches for a liquid ring whenever the gas is nasty. The price is the seal water system, and most of the Find-the-fault tree is really about keeping that water right.

Speed and the fan laws

Why a small speed change is a big deal.

Spin a blower or pump faster and three things go up, but not by the same amount. This is the picture behind the Speed change effect calculator. Drag the speed and watch the three bars race apart:

Speed
At 100% of the old speed: flow 1.00×, pressure 1.00×, power 1.00×
Try this: nudge the speed to 120%. Flow just follows along at 1.2 times, but pressure jumps to 1.44 and the power the motor must give leaps to 1.73. That is why a small speed rise can trip a motor, and a small speed drop saves a surprising amount of energy.
The rule: flow follows speed, pressure follows speed squared, power follows speed cubed. Same idea whether you change a pulley, a gearbox or a variable-speed drive.

Put real numbers to it in the Speed change effect tab under Size it, and change a pulley pair in the Belt & pulley speed tab.

How heat load works

Water carrying heat away.

A liquid ring pump makes heat, and the seal water carries it off. How much heat a water stream can carry comes down to two things: how much water flows, and how many degrees it warms up. Watch the water pick up heat as it passes:

Try this: double the flow, then instead double the warm-up. Either one doubles the heat carried. That is the whole rule: heat = flow × how much it warms (× water's heat capacity).
The rule: Heat (kW) = litres per second × 4.19 × degrees of warm-up. Cool water, or more of it, carries more heat.
